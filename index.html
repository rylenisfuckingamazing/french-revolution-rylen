Timeline of the French Revolution

1789: The Revolution Begins.

May 5, 1789: Estates-General convened (called to meet) Louis XVI called a meeting of the Estates-General to address the financial crisis. Each estate presented grievances (complaints).

June 17, 1789: Formation of the National Assembly The Third Estate declared itself the National Assembly, asserting (declaring) their role as the true representatives (those who speak for others) of the French people.

June 20, 1789: Tennis Court Oath Members of the National Assembly vowed (promised) not to disband (break up) until a new constitution (a written plan of government) was established.

July 14, 1789: Storming of the Bastille (The Bastille was a fortress in Paris, originally built in the 1300s to defend the city) This event symbolized (represented) the start of the revolution. The prison was stormed (attacked) to obtain (get) weapons and ammunition (supplies for guns), marking a shift (change) in power.

July 17, 1789: Great Fear begins Peasant revolts (rebellions) erupted (started suddenly) in the countryside, fueled (driven) by fears of noble reprisals (punishments) and rising bread prices.

August 4, 1789: Abolition of Feudal Privileges The National Assembly abolished (ended) feudalism (a system where nobles owned land and peasants worked it), ending the privileges of the first and second estates.

August 26, 1789: Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen This document proclaimed (announced) the principles (basic ideas) of liberty, equality, and fraternity, serving as a foundation (basis) for the new French Republic (government).

1790: Early Reforms

July 12, 1790: Civil Constitution of the Clergy This reform aimed (planned) to bring the Catholic Church under state control (government control), leading to tensions (conflicts) with the clergy.

1791: Growing Tensions

June 20-21, 1791: Flight to Varennes King Louis XVI (King Louis XVI was the last king of France before the French Revolution. He ruled from 1774 until 1792 and was known for his weak leadership and inability to address France's financial crisis.) and his family attempted (tried) to escape from Paris but were caught and returned, increasing suspicions about his loyalty to the revolution.

September 3, 1791: New Constitution adopted A constitutional monarchy (a system of government with a king whose powers are limited by laws) was established, but King Louis XVI retained (kept) significant (important) powers.

1792: Radicalization of the Revolution

April 20, 1792: France declares war on Austria The revolutionaries sought (looked for) to spread revolutionary ideas and faced opposition (resistance) from monarchies (kingdoms) in Europe.

August 10, 1792: Storming of the Tuileries The monarchy was effectively overthrown (removed from power), leading to the suspension (temporary halt) of Louis XVI.

September 2-7, 1792: September Massacres Fears of counter-revolution (opposition to the revolution) led to the execution (killing) of prisoners in Paris.

1793: The Reign of Terror

January 21, 1793: Execution of Louis XVI Louis was executed by guillotine (a device used for beheading), marking the end of the monarchy.

September 5, 1793 - July 28, 1794: Reign of Terror Led by Robespierre and the Committee of Public Safety, thousands were executed for perceived (believed) disloyalty to the revolution.

1794: Turning Point

July 27-28, 1794: Fall of Robespierre Robespierre was arrested and executed, leading to the end of the Reign of Terror.

1795: The Directory

October 5, 1795: Establishment of the Directory A new government formed, but it was marked by corruption (dishonesty) and inefficiency (lack of effectiveness)

1799: Rise of Napoleon

November 9, 1799 : Coup of 18 Brumaire Napoleon Bonaparte (French military leader and emperor) staged a coup (a sudden, illegal takeover), effectively ending the Revolution and establishing himself as the leader of France.

Conclusion

The French Revolution fundamentally changed (significantly altered) the course of French history, abolishing (ending) the monarchy, establishing a republic, and introducing ideals (principles) of liberty and equality. However, it also paved the way (cleared the path) for the rise of Napoleon and subsequent conflicts (wars) in Europe. The revolution had lasting effects, influencing movements (campaigns) for democracy (government by the people) and rights across the globe.
